Dough: Let's assume that producing the wheat flour for the dough, including cultivation, harvesting, milling, and transportation, emits approximately 0.5 kg of CO2 per kg of flour. For a medium-sized pizza, let's estimate around 300 grams of flour are used, so the CO2 emissions for the dough would be approximately 0.15 kg (300 g * 0.5 kg CO2/kg flour).Tomato Sauce: The production of tomato sauce, including tomato cultivation, processing, and transportation, might emit around 0.3 kg of CO2 per kg of sauce. If the pizza uses about 150 grams of tomato sauce, the CO2 emissions would be approximately 0.045 kg (150 g * 0.3 kg CO2/kg sauce).Cheese: Cheese production can vary widely depending on the type and source. Let's assume that producing mozzarella cheese emits around 3 kg of CO2 per kg of cheese. If the pizza uses about 150 grams of cheese, the CO2 emissions would be approximately 0.45 kg (150 g * 3 kg CO2/kg cheese).Prosciutto: Producing prosciutto emits a significant amount of CO2 due to pig farming, processing, and curing. Let's assume that producing prosciutto emits around 6 kg of CO2 per kg of meat. If the pizza uses about 50 grams of prosciutto, the CO2 emissions would be approximately 0.3 kg (50 g * 6 kg CO2/kg prosciutto). Adding up these estimates, the total CO2 emissions for making one prosciutto pizza would be approximately: 0.15 kg (dough) + 0.045 kg (tomato sauce) + 0.45 kg (cheese) + 0.3 kg (prosciutto) = 0.945 kg CO2

the value of 27 kg CO2 per kilogram of beef is an estimate based on lifecycle assessments (LCAs) and carbon footprint analyses conducted by researchers and organizations in the field of sustainability and environmental science. These analyses take into account various inputs and processes involved in beef production, including land use, feed production, methane emissions from livestock, energy use, and emissions from processing and transportation. While the specific carbon footprint of beef can vary depending on factors such as production methods, geographic location, and supply chain practices, 27 kg CO2 per kilogram of beef is a commonly cited average value that represents the emissions associated with conventional beef production methods in many regions. Since you eat beef once a week, the total number of portions consumed in a year is 52 (once a week for 52 weeks). Total CO2 emissions for the year = CO2 emissions per portion * Number of portions per year = 5.4 kg CO2/portion * 52 portions/year = 280.8 kg CO2/year. So, if you eat beef once a week for the whole year, the total CO2 emissions associated with beef consumption would be approximately 280.8 kilograms of CO2 emissions per year

Let's assume your device consumes an average of 100 watts while streaming Netflix.Electricity consumption = (100 watts / 1000) kW * 1 hour = 0.1 kWh. The carbon intensity of electricity varies depending on the energy sources used by your provider (e.g., coal, natural gas, renewables). Let's assume an average carbon intensity of 0.5 kg CO2 per kWh of electricity consumed, which is typical for regions with a mix of fossil fuels and renewables. Calculation of CO2 Emissions: CO2 emissions (in kilograms) = Electricity consumption (in kWh) * Carbon intensity (in kg CO2/kWh). CO2 emissions = 0.1 kWh * 0.5 kg CO2/kWh = 0.05 kg CO2. So, streaming Netflix for 1 hour on a device consuming 100 watts of electricity, with electricity supplied by a provider with a carbon intensity of 0.5 kg CO2 per kWh, would produce approximately 0.05 kilograms of CO2 emissions.Total CO2 Emissions for the Year: Total CO2 emissions = CO2 emissions per hour * Total hours in a year = 0.05 kg CO2/hour * 365 hours/year: 18.25 kg CO2/year
